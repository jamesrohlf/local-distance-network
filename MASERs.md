MASERs — Microwave Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation


WHAT IT IS

A maser is the radio-wavelength counterpart of a laser: stimulated
emission from population-inverted molecular clouds, observed against a
bright background. In cosmology, the relevant masers are 22-GHz water
masers in dense, warm gas clumps orbiting in the accretion disks of
nearby AGN. Very-Long-Baseline Interferometry (VLBI) resolves the
maser spots, measures their proper motions and line-of-sight
accelerations, and turns Keplerian disk geometry into a purely
geometric distance — no rungs, no standard candles. The Megamaser
Cosmology Project (MCP) has measured ~10 such systems; NGC 4258 (D
≈ 7.6 Mpc) is the gold-standard anchor.


ROLE IN H0DN

MASERs are one of the geometric anchors that pin the absolute
zero-point of the whole ladder. They appear in the baseline through
two data files: anchors.dat (NGC 4258 distance used to calibrate
Cepheids and TRGB) and megamaser.dat (additional MCP galaxies used as
direct H0 ties when in the Hubble flow). Variant V12 ("Baseline
without NGC 4258") drops the anchor entirely and forces the ladder to
rely on Gaia parallaxes and DEB-LMC alone; variant V15 drops only the
Hubble-flow MCP galaxies but keeps NGC 4258. The combined effect
quantifies how much of the H0 result depends on this single
geometric system.
